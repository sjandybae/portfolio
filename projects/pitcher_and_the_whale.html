Pitcher and the Whale

April 2020 | 2D Platformer

Main Role: Project Manager
Subroles: Programmer, Narrative Designer, Level Designer

  • Game Design

  • Leadership

  • Documentation

"Pitcher and the Whale" is a FREE game available to play on Steam!
Visit the store page for more information!

For the Ludum Dare 46 game jam, three friends and I worked remotely on a platformer game from design to submission within 72 hours. "Pitcher and the Whale" has the player control Pitcher, a little fox with a bucket exploring the mountainous area and bringing water back to a dehydrating whale.

One of my teammates had the most experience working with GameMaker Studio 2, so my role only involved some coding when I was not working on managing our team's tasks and production pipeline, organizing playtesting sessions, collaborating on level design, and documenting our progress.

Due to our love for this project, after the game jam ended, we continued to work on it for three months, adding more features and improvements, all to eventually meet our goal of publishing the game to Steam.

Ideation

As soon as we were introduced with the game jam's theme of ‚Äúkeep it alive‚Äù, our team used a digital whiteboard to brainstorm ideas that could work around our objective and pre-existing code towards making a platformer game. ‚ÄúBeached whale‚Äù ultimately stayed out of all of the ideas we explored, and we challenged the platformer convention of moving to a single destination by instead having multiple destinations, allowing for exploration, discovery, and strategizing as the player worked to bring water back to the beached whale to ‚Äúkeep it alive‚Äù.

As the game‚Äôs central figure was a whale and our team name was related to foxes, I suggested for us to use a vibrant, lighthearted vibe and aesthetic for our game, featuring animal characters, a playful narrative, and very minimal violence. This would greatly inform additional gameplay mechanics and elements as development proceeded.

Production

In order to utilize pre-existing code and one of our team members‚Äô pixel art skills, we used Gamemaker Studio 2 to make a simple, 2D platformer game. Since it was my first time using Gamemaker Studio 2, my involvement with the game programming was limited to GUI elements, balancing health and timer systems, and iterating on the level design.

I was pleasantly surprised to see how simple it was to use tilesets and colliders to quickly adjust parts of the level, especially since level design was crucial to a non-linear platformer like this game, and playtesting revealed weaknesses and soft locks in its progression.

Since we were all working remotely, all of our collaboration was done through a Discord server and GitHub. As the sole team member with experience using GitHub, I explained its features to my team and was frequently involved with ensuring everyone‚Äôs work was merged without issues. Since our team was highly motivated and coming up with various ideas for features and improvements, I also used my experience working with spreadsheets to organize, prioritize, and delegate all of the tasks we would need to complete in sequence to deliver a playable product for playtesting, and then the final submission at the end of the three day game jam.

Iteration

Playtesting returned good feedback that made us direct all of our remaining time on improving the level design, especially in that we needed to equalize every pool of water‚Äôs distance from the whale, create secret shortcuts to discover, and add enemies to make returning buckets of water more fun and challenging. This resulted in our final game submission being surprisingly well-received, and many participants of the game jam left very positive feedback for our game‚Äôs physics, aesthetics, and twist on the platformer genre.

Publishing to Steam

Our success with this game led our team to agree on continuing development on our game, specifically towards a formal release on Steam. I continued my role managing the project, including setting deadlines, signing paperwork for Valve, and marketing our game on social media.

The three months leading up to the Steam release were essentially an extension of our game jam work, especially since we discussed and implemented significant game design changes such as new abilities, power-ups, and win-lose conditions. I also did a little more programming work by creating the menu and cutscene systems, while we commissioned other creatives to work on music and logo art.

Reflections

While my involvement was mainly managerial, it was incredibly rewarding for me to see the physics and art assets made by my teammates receive praise. As the first game I have ever formally published, it was an amazing feeling to see people post reviews, make playthrough videos, and basically acknowledge our work as a real part of game consumer culture.

An important lesson we learned from this project, however, is on the limitations of passion. While we all wanted to make this game the best it could be for Steam, it was ultimately a passion project seperated from education and employment, and we had to make some compromises in order to let it reach completion.

Nevertheless, we all took great pride in this achievement and agreed that "Pitcher and the Whale" would be the first of many games we will make in our respective gamemaking careers.
