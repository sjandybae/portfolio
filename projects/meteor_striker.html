Meteor Striker

December 2019 | 2.5D Action Platformer

Main Role: Lead Programmer
Subroles: Project Manager, Narrative Designer

  • Game Design

  • Leadership

  • Storytelling

  • Unity

  • Documentation

For an advanced game design course, I worked with three peers to make a Unity-based game that would challenge the conventions of an existing game genre, which in our case was platforming. The result of 4 months of design and development was "Meteor Striker", a game where you run and jump around the map to get mysterious swords, and use them to destroy incoming meteors before they hit the ground.

Being the most experienced with Unity programming, I created almost all of the C# scripts for the game, including both platforming physics and state-machine logic. I also acted as team leader, managing our team's production pipeline, defining our core game design and narrative, and documenting these processes as part of our course's requirements.

Ideation

During brainstorming, our team’s personal interests aligned greatly over Japanese anime culture, and we wanted to create a game experience that would have the player perform miraculous, borderline-ridiculous feats and feel that satisfaction repeatedly. We eventually settled on the idea of an action-platformer that would have the player destroy meteors threatening all of humanity using mere swords.

To challenge the platforming convention of reaching a destination, our project took inspiration from “Resogun” in that the level is circular, and core gameplay elements we derived through this layout is that the objective is to “protect the land” by finding swords and meteors through any form or direction of platforming. In other words, the gameplay was intended to incorporate skill and strategy, especially in grabbing swords in difficult-to-reach locations while meteors are not yet an immediate danger, and saving certain swords for unique meteors that can only be destroyed by them.

Process

While others worked on art assets and sub-features of our game, I was responsible for the core functionality of the platforming and meteor busting gameplay, using my Unity knowledge to make prototypes immediately for our team to review and iterate on. Using GitHub as our main collaborative tool, I continued to develop mechanics and features while my teammates’ art assets would get merged as soon as they were integrated for Unity on a dedicated art branch.

As an aspiring narrative designer, I also planned the game’s characters and setting, especially for tutorialization, giving narrative weight to gameplay events, and offering additional story as a reward for progression through our three levels. I wrote scripts for the cutscenes between levels, organized voice acting, and made character profiles for sprite artists we commissioned work for.

Iteration

For every major milestone, we had playtesting sessions that would reveal significant issues with our mostly experimental mechanics. For example, since the game ends if any meteor hits the ground, UI intended to point the player towards meteors went through several iterations before we eventually created a simple minimap. We were also lacking in tutorialization with our unorthodox controls and objective, and addressed it with instructional screens and voice narration.

However, certain fundamental issues could not be resolved before the final submission, the most pressing of which being level design. Players old and new to the platforming genre struggled with the physics of the player’s movement, and the platforms of the levels often required very precise timing, frequently leading into loops of frustration and failure before the player would give up and try somewhere else.

Reflections

Despite these issues, our game’s premise and core loop was well-received by the judges of our final game showcase, receiving the runner-up prize. While there were additional features and improvements our team would have liked to make, the biggest takeaway I got from watching guests play our game was that we should have placed much earlier thought into the level design, especially since our gameplay involved moving solely within the contained space. Nevertheless, we were very satisfied to see at least a few people complete our whole game, and appreciate the environment shaders and the story cutscenes with expressive character art.
