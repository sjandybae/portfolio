Rocket Warp

April 2014 | Endless Runner Game

Main Role: Game Designer + Programmer

For an introductory game design class, one of the last assignnments was to demonstrate all of the programming techniques we were taught to do in Java Processing, especially object-oriented programming and class inheritance.

Brainstorming

With only 48 hours to work, we had to quickly decide on the game's concept with a brainstorming session that took place over a Discord voice call. Some of our ideas included physically linked playable characters and defensive towers sharing a power grid. We eventually decided to explore commandable allies commonly seen in real-time strategy games, simplified to a core loop where the player must recruit allies to conquer enemy groups with sheer force of numbers.

Development

With very little time, each of us immediately began working on the major assets required for the concept: pixel art, ally AI behaviour, and--from personal interest--procedural map generation. By the first day, we were able to get all required sprites, an algorithm that randomly creates trees and hills with colliders for us, and basic ally units that would follow the player.

Early in the second day, we began incorporating the ability to command ally units to move to a target marker, placeable via mouse click. The main challenge we encountered was getting the allies to continue to not overlap each other (to maintain the image of being a large army) while still getting within range to attack enemies with their short melee attacks.

The foundation of other important features were also completed during the second day. This includes adding enemy camps to the procedural map generation, effectively allowing for various difficulties with larger groups of enemies naturally needing more allies to successfully conquer.

The map generation was also updated to have random ally units scattered around to be "collected" by the player avatar in order to test recruitment, but this was ultimately kept in the final submission because while we were able to get "ghost" units working, we did not have time to get defeated enemy camps to convert into new allies--as written in our initial game design.

The original game design also detailed three uniquely distinct types of allies: sword users, bow users, and magic users. As such, we also created a toggle for the marker system to handle commanding each of these groups to fight enemies they would have an advantage against. However, this toggle was omitted from the final submission since we were unable to get projectiles such as arrows and magic working in time.

Reflections

As described in the above section, several game features were unable to be implemented within the 48 hours we were given, and we had to settle for adjusting the build to be a demo of our concept of commandable allies in a procedurally generated world. It was ambitious of us to attempt to create high-level technical features like procedural map generation and AI unit behaviour when most of us were new to Godot. Much of our 48 hours were spent researching, testing, and tuning these unfamiliar algorithms to serve our purposes.

Our team agreed that this game jam was a good lesson in setting scale and planning properly, especially when we need to include time to ramp up our skills. Nevertheless, our incomplete submission received some positive feedback for our overall aesthetic and concept, so we ended the project confident that the game design has potential given enough time.
