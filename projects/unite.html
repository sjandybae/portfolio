Unite

June 2021 | 2D Strategy Game

Main Role: Project Manager
Subroles: Programmer, QA

#WebBrowser #Godot #GameDesign #GameJam#48Hours

  • Game Design

  • Leadership

  • Design Docs

Our submission for the GMTK 2021 Game Jam can be found here!

For a 48 hour game jam, two peers and I challenged ourselves to build a game entirely from scratch using the game engine, Godot. With the theme "Joined Together", we brainstormed and agreed on a concept where the player collects and commands a swarm of fighting units that must grow bigger and bigger to take on tougher enemies and bosses.

With one of my peers handling the main Godot programming and the other making pixel art for the whole game, I primarily contributed my skills in project management, being the team member most familiar with Git repositories.

Ideation

With only 48 hours to work, we had to quickly decide on the game's concept with a brainstorming session that took place over a Discord voice call. Some of our ideas included playable characters moving while physically linked together and setting up defensive towers on a shared power grid. We eventually decided to explore commandable allies commonly seen in real-time strategy games, simplified to a core loop where the player must recruit allies to conquer enemy groups with sheer force of numbers.

Environment Design

After I completed the C# scripts needed for the viewer to move around a body of water, our team worked collectively to design the abandoned city the viewer will explore using mostly free assets available on the Unity Store. Since it was important that there was a clear path to where we wanted the viewer to see the ending, the buildings were arranged somewhat unrealistically to create a large forward path that could be followed to reach the final destination.

However, there were also four “clues” in the form of objects that, when examined, play clips of dialogue that expand on the narrative and clarify the eventual ending. Since we wanted to generate a sense of discovery with these clues, we put them in separate side paths branching from the main path so the viewer would have to explore to find them. To facilitate discovery of these clues, we gave each of them looping sound effects such as car alarms or radio songs that would get louder as the viewer approached them.

Iteration

Feedback we received during the first stages of development generally involved the environment’s layout. Our testers tended to get lost in the city, unsure of what our “main” path was, and reached areas that were not meant to be accessible. To resolve these issues, one of my team members added flashing lights to streetlights that were part of the main path, and I created barricades for openings between buildings surrounding the main and side paths, using objects such as flipped cars or cabinets to keep them interesting and relevant to the disaster-themed narrative.

Other issues we encountered included frame rate drops, motion sickness, and glitches when traversing the obstacle ridden waters. Although I was able to adjust the scripts to handle some glitches, we were unable to do much about the motion sickness as our narrative setting required the water to have mid-sized waves, which we discovered causes seasickness with or without the boat bobbing up and down with them.

Reflections

The project was a great success at our class’s public showcase, especially due to our installation’s use of smelly beach tarps and a real inflatable boat to facilitate sensory immersion. Our teaching staff were particularly impressed by the implementation of a deep, engaging narrative about climate change, and the use of VR’s immersiveness to generate strong emotional reactions towards this real-world issue.

As the course was only seven weeks long and my first time working with Unity, I had to learn a new creative software fast enough to contribute to a big project. Fortunately, my team members were very diligent and capable, and in any other team, I may have struggled to meet the course’s requirements. I am grateful to have had the opportunity to work with and exchange trust between such talented peers.
