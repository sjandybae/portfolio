The Park In-between

October 2021 | 3D First Person Shooter

Main Role: Game Designer
Subroles: Project Manager, Git Manager, Level Designer

Our submission for the Spooky Jam 2021 can be found here!

For a 7 day Halloween-themed game jam, two peers and I remotely teamed up to make a simple game using the Godot engine. The end result was a simple 3D first-person shooter game where the player must navigate a mysterious, dark area haunted by deadly ghosts to find keys that will open the way to freedom.

I was invited to join as a game designer, especially to use my project management and Git repo management skills to bring our team's collective work into a unified and complete game for our portfolios.

Brainstorming

Brainstorming around the announced theme of “Afterlife”, we mostly considered core loops and mechanics centered around reincarnation, specifically in that your player avatar can “die” in the game, but this leads to the game continuing instead of ending. However, we backtracked upon realizing two things: our team’s overall familiarity with Godot was still new, and setting up other game elements to support the "fun" of a concept where the player must seek ways to essentially "lose" the game was out of scope. We ultimately decided to explore Godot’s 3D capabilities by creating a basic first-person shooter and expanding from there.

Development

Development began immediately after brainstorming, with each of us ramping up skills needed for the rest of development. I created the Git repo and gave an overview on how everyone's work will be connected and managed, while the rest of the team worked on the first person movement to introduce Godot to our team member who was new to the engine.

After completing the first person movement, we had another conversation to determine how to proceed with making the "game". However, it was only after we completed a fully functional terrain when we finally landed on a game concept featuring open exploration to find multiple "keys", especially since we were considering other ideas such surviving waves of enemies or following a linear route with a finish line.

From there, I recommended the team split into making basic required features (i.e. shooting and killing enemies, picking up items, GUI) and advanced creative features (i.e. "flat 3D" objects, rendering filters, enemy movement via NavMesh pathfinding) based on each teammate's interests and experience using Godot. This decision resulted in the whole team's familiarity with Godot and the project's specific code and assets getting synchronized in the first two days, which I would later find was essential in bringing the project to completion in the final stretch.

In the final two days, I also got involved in the game's development by sourcing and implementing some sound effects and 3D assets we were lacking.

Level Design

The biggest obstacle we encountered late in development is that while all the features and objects were ready, there was no level design to help guide players towards the locations of the various keys. My idea was to have arrows on the ground discoverable with the flashlight, and we attempted to implement a level design based on the terrain's layout. However, we realized early that it would be a long and difficult process due to the terrain's massive size.

With about 24 hours remaining, our teammate newest to Godot stepped up and created a level design from scratch by using simple planes and measuring how far the player can see with the flashlight at any given time. We then adjusted the terrain around this level design prototype, resulting in a solid game players can explore freely, yet encounter specific "scary" situations, each carefully thought out with the placement of the key, its arrows, and the surrounding walls and enemies.

Reflections

As the "game designer" of the project, I gained significant insight into the meaning of this role when the team reflected on the past week after our submission. We agreed that the level design should have been considered much earlier, but also that as game designer, it should have been up to me to pitch the initial game concept based on everyone's interests. Since it was a game jam, I felt it was important to make sure everyone was having fun making what they wanted to make, but admittedly, this resulted in a very disorganized process as we each acted on our own ideas for technical features and gameplay moments.

Nevertheless, we were all very proud of what we accomplished, and I am confident I made smart decisions that brought us here. Having our less experienced team member build the basic project allowed him to collaborate confidently with our more advanced programmer, and the project may not have been submitted in time if I did not set the scope, reject extraneous feature ideas, and define development priorities throughout the development pipeline.
